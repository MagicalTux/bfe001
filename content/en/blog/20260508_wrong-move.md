---
title: "Wrong move"
slug: "wrong-move"
date: 2026-05-08
description: "Age verification mandates and the war on VPNs aren't going to do what their advocates think they will."
---

The EU, the UK, and a growing list of other governments are
tightening age-verification rules for online services, and several
have started treating VPNs as the next thing to push against. The
framing is protection: children shouldn't be reaching the worst
corners of the internet, and platforms should be the ones stopping
them. It sounds reasonable until you look at what the policy
actually does.

Society has always shared the burden of age-gating. Bartenders check
ID, cinemas refuse minors, shopkeepers ask for proof. The question
isn't whether strangers should ever enforce age limits — they always
have — it's whether this particular way of enforcing them online is
worth what it costs.

A bouncer glancing at a driver's licence doesn't create a database.
An online age-verification system usually does. Even when the data
is "only" handled by a third party and "only" stored briefly, the
cost is permanent: every adult who wants to read or watch something
is now either entering identity data into some chain of systems, or
being pushed toward whatever workaround is currently fashionable.
That cost is borne every day, by everyone, to deter a population
that will route around it within an afternoon.

Because they will. When I was thirteen, somebody at school always
had a porn magazine and was perfectly happy to share it with the
entire class. Kids who want to find something will find it; the only
thing that changes generation to generation is the route. A
13-year-old today needs about thirty seconds and a free VPN app to
be back where they started. The kids the law is aimed at are the
ones least constrained by it. The people actually constrained are
non-technical adults — the audience the law was never about.

What would actually help is unglamorous: better device-level
controls, real digital literacy in schools, and support for the
parents who can't or don't engage with their kids' online lives. Set
the limits at the source. Expect them to be broken. Scold the kid
when they are, and treat the broken limit as part of the education
rather than evidence the cage needs to be tougher. Kids whose
parents have those conversations turn out fine. Kids whose parents
don't won't be fixed by a government-mandated ID check on the other
end of the wire.

The reason no minister picks that approach is that it's expensive,
slow, and invisible. Funding parenting support and digital-literacy
curricula shows results, maybe, in twenty years. Mandating age
verification and declaring war on VPNs lets you hold a press
conference next week. Every government picks the same way every
time, and the people who end up paying for it are the adults who
weren't doing anything wrong.

Protecting children is a real goal. This isn't how you do it.
